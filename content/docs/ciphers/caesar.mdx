## Overview

The Caesar cipher is a **substitution cipher** where each letter in the plaintext is shifted by a fixed number of positions down the alphabet.

$$E_n(x) = (x + n) \bmod 26$$

$$D_n(x) = (x - n) \bmod 26$$

Where $x$ is the position of the letter (0–25) and $n$ is the shift (the key).

## Example

With a shift of 3:

- Plaintext: `HELLO`
- Ciphertext: `KHOOR`

## Cryptanalysis

The Caesar cipher is trivial to break:

- Only 25 possible keys (26 including shift 0)
- Can be broken by brute force or frequency analysis
- Letter frequency patterns remain unchanged

## Why It Matters

Despite its weakness, the Caesar cipher introduces fundamental concepts: key space, substitution, and the importance of large key spaces in cryptographic security.
