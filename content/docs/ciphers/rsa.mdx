## Overview

RSA is an **asymmetric cryptosystem** based on the practical difficulty of factoring the product of two large prime numbers. It was described in 1977 by Ron Rivest, Adi Shamir, and Leonard Adleman.

## Key Generation

1. Choose two large distinct primes $p$ and $q$
2. Compute $n = p \times q$
3. Compute $\phi(n) = (p-1)(q-1)$ (Euler's totient)
4. Choose a public exponent $e$ such that $1 < e < \phi(n)$ and $\gcd(e, \phi(n)) = 1$
5. Compute the private exponent $d$ where $d \equiv e^{-1} \pmod{\phi(n)}$

The **public key** is $(e, n)$ and the **private key** is $(d, n)$.

## Encryption

$$ C = M^e \bmod n $$

Where $M$ is the plaintext (as an integer $0 < M < n$) and $C$ is the ciphertext.

## Decryption

$$ M = C^d \bmod n $$

The correctness follows from Euler's theorem:

$$ M^{ed} \equiv M \pmod{n} $$

## Security

RSA security relies on the **factoring problem**: given $n$, find $p$ and $q$. The best known algorithms (GNFS, Number Field Sieve) have sub-exponential complexity:

$$ L_n[1/3, (64/9)^{1/3}] \approx e^{1.9(\ln n)^{1/3}(\ln \ln n)^{2/3}} $$

Current recommendations use 2048-bit or 4096-bit keys.

## Attacks

- **Textbook RSA** is deterministic and malleable — always use OAEP padding
- **Small exponent attacks** — if $e$ is small and $M$ is small, $M^e < n$ and simple integer root extraction recovers $M$
- **Timing attacks** — mitigated with constant-time exponentiation
- **Quantum** — Shor's algorithm factors $n$ in polynomial time on a quantum computer
